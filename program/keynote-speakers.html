Keynote 1 : Design and Implementation of Stereo Matching for Depth Estimation in
                     Computer Vision Applications

Professor Liang-Gee Chen, National Taiwan University, Taiwan 

09:00-10:00, Monday, 14 December 2015

Abstract:   
Depth information is the essential enabler of many emerging computer vision applications, such as 3D model reconstruction, augmented/virtual reality, computational photography, and pose estimation/recognition for human-machine interface. Although active sensing techniques provide accurate depth maps and have been adopted in many commercial devices, they are usually constrained to indoor applications for their short sensing range and vulnerability to the sun light. Also, their limited resolution and huge power consumption are still remaining challenges for portable consumer electronics. In contrast, the passive methods using stereo matching have the opportunity to provide high-resolution depth maps, even for outdoor or low-power scenarios. Recently, many research works also showed that great disparity accuracy can be achieved by using multi-view light field systems, instead of conventional two-view cameras.

Stereo matching problems have been widely studied in computer vision, and many excellent algorithms are also available. They use the correspondence between RGB images to estimate the disparity of each pixel which is inversely proportional to the depth. However, to bring high-quality disparity maps into our daily life, we will need efficient hardware designs to provide real-time, cost-effective and low-power processing. In this keynote, I will review previous achievements on this topic and also explore new challenges and opportunities. The three key ingredients of stereo matching will be discussed: local cost generation, global energy optimization and post-processing. Design considerations for different camera systems, e.g. two-view v.s. light-field, will also be highlighted.

Speaker's Biography
Professor Liang-Gee Chen received the B.S., M.S., and Ph.D. degrees in electrical engineering from National Cheng Kung University, Tainan, Taiwan, R.O.C. in 1979, 1981, and 1986, respectively. In 1988, he joined the Department of Electrical Engineering, National Taiwan University. Recently, he serves as the Executive Vice-President of National Taiwan University. He received the National Professorship of Taiwan. He is the IEEE Fellow from 2001. His research interests include DSP architecture design, video processor design, and video coding systems. Dr. Chen has served as an Associate Editor of several IEEE Transactions including Circuits and Systems for Video Technology, VLSI Systems, Circuits and Systems II. He served as the TPC chair of 2009 IEEE ICASSP and ISCAS 2012. In 2011, he received the Best paper award of IEEE CICC, Custom Integrated Circuits Conference .

Keynote 2:  Computer Vision: From Recognition to Understanding

Dr. Yong Rui, Microsoft Research Asia, China 

09:00-10:00, Tuesday, 15 December 2015

Abstract:   
Since the very beginning of AI, computer vision has been a key enabling technology. In this talk, we will first review computer vision’s 50 years of progress.  We will highlight key milestones on good features, SVMs, and DNNs. We will also cover recent key progresses in image classification, detection and semantic segmentation. Moving from image recognition to image understanding, we will specifically discuss recent achievements in how to use deep learning and language model to generate close-to-human quality sentences from images and videos.  In conclusion, we show there are still many challenges in all the above discussed tasks and rally the researchers to bring the image/video understanding research to the next level.

Keynote 3: Robust Hashing and its Applications to Content Identification and Retrieval 

Professor Pierre Moulin, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, USA

09:00-10:00, Wednesday, 16 December 2015

Abstract:   
The need to compress signals (audio, video, etc.) into short bitstrings for the purpose of reliable identification (instead of reconstruction) arises in many modern applications, including management of user-submitted content websites such as YouTube; connected audio, e.g. the Shazam application running on smartphones; and contextual advertising.  These short bitstrings are called robust hashes, or fingerprints of the signal.  A successful application of content identification requires that different versions of a signal can be matched reliably based on their fingerprints (a property called robustness) whereas unrelated signals yield a negative match (this property is called discriminability).  This talk will overview recent hash constructions based on machine learning.  The applicability of these hashes to image retrieval applications will also be discussed.

After working as a Research Scientist for Bell Communications Research in Morristown, New Jersey, he joined the University of Illinois as Assistant Professor(1996) and later became Associate Professor (1999) and Professor (2003) in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Research Professor in the Coordinated Science Laboratory, faculty member in the Beckman Institute's Image Formation and Processing Group, and affiliate professor in the department of Statistics. He is also a member of the Advanced Digital Sciences Center, a multidisciplinary research center of UIUC in Singapore. His fields of professional interest are information theory, image and video processing, statistical signal processing and modeling, decision theory, information hiding and authentication, and the application of multiresolution signal analysis, optimization theory, and fast algorithms to these areas.

He served as Associate Editor for the IEEE Transactions on Information Theory from 1996 till 1998, for the IEEE Transactions on Image Processing from 1999 till 2002, and then as Area Editor from 2002 till 2006. He is a Fellow of IEEE (2003), recipient of a 1997 Career award from the National Science Foundation, and of the IEEE Signal Processing Society 1997 Best Paper award in the IMDSP area. He was on the Dean's list of teachers rated excellent by their students in 1996, 1999, 2000, 2005, 2007, 2009, 2011, 2012, and 2014.
