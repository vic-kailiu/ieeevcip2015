TA1:         High Efficiency Video Coding – Coding Tools and Specification: HEVC Version 2 and
                 Beyond
Speaker: Mathias Wien, RWTH Aachen University, Germany

Time:       09:00-12:00

Abstract:   
The tutorial covers the complete HEVC standard, including the so-far adopted as well as arising extensions (range extensions, scalability, multi-view, 3D video coding, and screen content coding). The standard is assessed from various perspectives, including an algorithmic view on the video coding layer as well as a high-level / system-layer view on the network abstraction layer and the overall structure. The discussion includes a detailed treatment of the HEVC layer concept which allows for seamless incorporation of spatial and quality scalability as well as multi-view and 3D extensions. The provision of this flexible concept has been adopted already in Version 1 of HEVC. It now reveals its full benefit in the definition of the mentioned extensions. 

The essential concepts and the coding tools comprised in each of the extensions are detailed and explained in the context of their respective application space. The tutorial further discusses the basic structure of specification text from a more abstract point of view as well as by concrete example in HEVC. For all mentioned perspectives, the tutorial develops the topic in a step-by-step fashion and gradually introduces concepts, algorithms, and terminology. Examples are provided at all levels of the presentation illustrating the concepts and deepening the understanding of the presented technology. 

The tutorial shall enable the participants to understand the design principles and concepts behind the specification of HEVC. They shall recognize and understand the innovation of HEVC compared to the previous standards (esp. H.264/AVC) and regard the extensible nature of the specification design. The tutorial shall educate the participants to further explore related knowledge resources and, specifically, the specification text itself.

TA2:           Regularities of Visual Data and Their Applications
Speakers: Shenghua Gao, ShanghaiTech University, China
                   Kui Jia, Advanced Digital Sciences Center, Singapore
                   Tianzhu Zhang, Chinese Academy of Sciences, China
                   Weisheng Dong, Xidian University, China

Time:         09:00-12:00

Abstract:   
This tutorial aims to present the very recent breakthroughs in discovering of high dimensional data structure and their applications in computer vision and image processing research. These results are achieved by leveraging new powerful, more advanced sparse representation models and by developing efficient large-scale optimization algorithms.

The tutorial will start with introducing the methods for modeling the regularities of visual data in computer vision, including the basic theory of sparse/low-rank recovery, with its generalization to more advanced sparse models which considers other structures of the data.   We introduce these concepts in the context of signal processing and from the perspectives of geometric intuition, formal definition, and recovery conditions, with motivating applications. Following introduction of basic theory, we present choices of algorithms that can better cope with the issues of non-smoothness and scale, commonly occurring in the large-scale optimization problems of sparsity/low-rank models. Intrinsic low-dimensional structures are among the most fundamental phenomena in natural images due to rich local regularities, global symmetries, repetitive textures, or redundant sampling. This tutorial will continue with presenting the very recent breakthroughs in image processing. These results are essentially obtained by properly harnessing these rich low-dimensional structures prevailing in natural images, using carefully designed more advanced models with sparsity/low-rank constriants, for various low-level vision tasks. We will specially show that how local sparsity/low-rank models and non-local self-similarity of natural images are exploited to achieve these breakthrough results. Intrinsic low-dimensional structures are not limited to low-level image processing. High-level semantic concepts, which may be hidden in massive high-dimensional and less organized visual data, can also be discovered by learning low-dimensional invariant representations of the concepts of interest. In the third session of this tutorial, we will present striking results recently obtained in computer vision research. We cover a variety of mainstream vision applications ranging from face/object recognition, object alignment, feature correspondence/matching, tracking, to unsupervised object discovery and ambiguous learning. In the fourth part, we will also discuss how to leverage the regularities of visual data for video tracking tasks.

TP1:           HEVC Screen Content Coding (SCC) – Standardization and Technologies  
Speakers: Ji-Zheng Xu, Microsoft Research Asia, China
                   Wen-Hsiao Peng, National Chiao Tung University, Taiwan

Time:        14:00-17:00

Abstract:   
Nowadays, more and more visual information is rendered from a computer or an electronic device rather than a camera. Such type of visual information, which can be in the form of either images or video, is often referred to as screen content. Many emerging applications—e.g. wireless projector, remote desktop, cloud gaming, remote diagnoses and remote education—call for efficient processing and transmission of such content. Recognizing the demand for an industry standard for coding of screen content, the ISO/IEC Moving Picture Experts Group and ITU-T Video Coding Experts Group have since January 2014 been developing new extensions for HEVC, with the aim of enhancing significantly its capability of coding screen content. This tutorial is to provide a timely introduction to the core technologies in this new standard and its applications. Specifically, it will cover (1) an overview of SCC standardization activities for HEVC and its extensions, (2) the challenges and issues in designing SCC algorithms, (3) the core technologies--such as transform-skip, intra block copy, palette mode, hash-based search, and adaptive color transform--that are currently in the draft standard or are being studied for potential inclusion, as well as (4) the recent implementations of SCC systems/applications.

TP2:         Cloud Communications for Highly Interactive Multimedia Communications
Speaker: Anil Fernando, University of Surrey, UK

Time:       14:00-17:00

Abstract:   
Due to the emerge of various media resources available via high speed Internet connection, it is witnessed a media revolution with ever increasing quality expectations. According to CISCO by the end of 2015, Internet video will reach 62% of consumer Internet traffic and the number of all forms of video will be 90% approximately. In order to be responsive to the demands of what this revolution brings, mechanisms handling the needs for massive computing power, storage and bandwidth have to be addressed worldwide. The state-of-the-art systems consider the integration of cloud computing, media processing and delivery strategies all together forming media cloud to overcome such a challenge. In traditional content delivery mechanisms, there are two approaches which are client-server and peer-to-peer (P2P). In client-server approach, a server having the content distributes to all other clients. Although client-server approach is a convenient way for content delivery, it is insufficient in addressing time and bandwidth demands of live content delivery. In P2P transmission, media is shared by a number of peers either through a tree or mesh network model between peers and enable higher data transfer rate via parallel downloads. Although the transmission via P2P network increases the throughput and addresses the scalability issues of client-server model, the peers’ limited resources such as bandwidth and the unpredictability of the peers’ availability are the main concerns for the high quality media delivery. Besides, P2P is not well suited to synchronous delivery of live streams due to possible spread of peers around the world and their highly varying network conditions. To address the issues described above, Content delivery through Content Delivery Networks (CDN) has become popular among the traditional content delivery mechanisms due to the increase of users’ quality expectations. However, the increased demand for high quality media and the availability of network resources such as bandwidth have increased the quality expectations of end users. Due to this trend, CDN approach has become insufficient in addressing the intensive processing needs and fast transfer of high quality media especially in live streaming cases. In order to meet new challenges in media communications, Media Clouds can be considered for media by not only using cloud resources for storage and processing of the content but also for the delivery of the content. In this way Media Clouds provide a dynamic environment enabling optimization of available virtualized cloud resources in content processing and delivery jointly which are some of the key requirements for future interactive media applications. Therefore in this tutorial, attendees will have the opportunity to understand the state-of-the-art cloud communications architecture and the future cloud infrastructure requirements for interactive media communications. Tutorial will consider how clouds can help in future interactive media communication applications.
